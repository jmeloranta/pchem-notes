\otitle{1.3 The ideal gas temperature scale}

\otext
Following the ideas of the previous section, we define the ideal gas temperature:

\aeqn{1.4}{T = \lim\limits_{P\rightarrow 0} \frac{P\bar{V}}{R} = \lim\limits_{P\rightarrow 0} \frac{PV}{nR}}

The unit of thermodynamical temperature is Kelvin (1 / 273.16 of the temperature of the triple point of water). Conversion between different temperature units:

\aeqn{1.5a}{t = T - 273.15\textnormal{ (Celsius degrees (\textit{t}) and Kelvin degrees (\textit{T})}}

\aeqn{1.5b}{t = \frac{9}{5}\left(T - 273.15 \right) + 32\textnormal{ (Farenheit degrees (\textit{t}) and Kelvin degrees (\textit{T})}}

Also note that the SI unit of pressure $P$ is pascal (Pa; N / m$^2$). The atmospheric pressure is 101,325 Pa. Other units of pressure are atmospheres (atm) and bar.

\textbf{Common conversion factors.} In Kelvin units, the energy corresponds to $kT$. Note also that 1 K = 3.1669 $\times$ 10$^{-6}$ Hartree (atomic units; a. u.) or 1 eV = 27.2114 Hartree. To get J mol$^{-1}$ or kcal mol$^{-1}$, multiply by Avogadro's number $N_A$. For example, 1 eV = 1.602177$\times$10$^{-19}$ J and 1 atm = 760 torr.\\
